# Bio-bounce:  a platformer game that showcases the essence of synthetic biology for all ages
We have partnered with team Fudan and NUDT-CHINA to co-develop a synthetic biology-themed game,named Bio-bounce. This game is not only fun and engaging but also designed to convey the core concepts of synthetic biology in an accessible way.

![Bio-bounce](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/game/2025-10-05-135122.webp)

^Gameplay Concept:^

The player controls a small character on a perilous journey, aiming to collect as much energy as possible and ultimately reach the destination. However, extreme environments—intense heat and freezing cold—pose serious challenges. This is where genes with different functions come into play. When the character acquires the “anti-freeze gene,” she gains the ability to withstand cold conditions. Conversely, when facing extreme heat, she must first use “gene scissors” to remove the anti-freeze gene before obtaining the “heat-resistance gene” to adapt to the high temperatures. It’s important to note that if she accidentally encounters the scissors while still in a cold zone, she will lose her cold resistance, making the journey even more treacherous.

![](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/game/game/game/bio1.webp)
![](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/game/game/game/bio2.webp)
^Art Design:^

The game’s platforms are modeled after various cellular organelles, set against a warm, flesh-toned background. This artistic approach immerses players in a vibrant microscopic world, subtly deepening their understanding of synthetic biology as they play.

![Online discussion](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/game/online-dis.webp)

^Summary:^

We collaborated to create a synthetic biology-themed game that combines entertainment with scientific education. Players guide a character through a hazardous microscopic world, using functional genes like “anti-freeze” and “heat-resistance” to adapt to extreme temperatures. The game creatively translates gene editing mechanisms into engaging gameplay—such as using “gene scissors” to remove genes when necessary. Set against visually rich cellular landscapes with warm-toned backgrounds, the experience fosters an intuitive and immersive understanding of synthetic biology in a subtle yet impactful way.

# Science and Technology Week: Synthetic Biology for Children

In line with our commitment to fostering scientific curiosity and promoting synthetic biology education among younger generations, our team participated in the University of Science and Technology of China (USTC) Science and Technology Week on May 17, 2025. We designed and delivered a series of interactive and educational activities titled "Life's New Code: A First Experience with Synthetic Biology", aimed at introducing children to the fascinating world of synthetic biology.

^1. Overview^

	We set up an engaging booth at the West Campus of USTC, featuring hands-on experiments, interactive games, and visual demonstrations. Using vivid analogies and engaging visuals, we simplified complex biological concepts to make them accessible and captivating for young minds. Our goal was to make synthetic biology approachable and fun, helping children build a foundational understanding of genetic engineering, microbial applications, and the basic principles of life sciences.

![Booth Setup at USTC Science Week](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/children-education/figure-1.webp)

![Educational Posters for Synthetic Biology Introduction](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/children-education/figure-2.webp)

^2 Key Activities^

	^a.Fluorescent Bacterial Art Presentation^

		We displayed petri dishes with fluorescent E. coli engineered to form colorful patterns such as Doraemon and "USTC". This visual showcase attracted many children and served as an entry point to explain gene expression and microbial engineering.

![Children Gathering Around Fluorescent Bacterial Art Display](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/children-education/figure-3.webp)

	^b.DNA Puzzle Game^

		Using a physical DNA model kit comprising puzzle pieces representing A, T, G, C, deoxyribose, and phosphate groups, we explained the double-helix structure of DNA and the principle of complementary base pairing. The children were then invited to construct the complementary DNA strand by selecting and assembling the correct molecular components, thereby reinforcing their understanding of molecular genetics through hands-on, tactile learning.

![Team Member Explaining DNA Structure with Physical Model](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/children-education/figure-5.webp)

	^c.Hands-on Experiments^

		We demonstrated a simple yet revealing experiments,Calcein Color Change: Illustrating ion chelation and chemical sensing.

	^d.Interactive Q&A and Lecture Sessions^

		Using roll-the-wheel games and poster explanations, we engaged children in topics related to plants, human biology, and microorganisms. Correct answers were rewarded with souvenirs, encouraging active participation. 

![Interactive Mini-Lecture on Synthetic Biology Principles](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/children-education/figure-7.webp)

^3. Impact and Reflection^

	Prior to the event, our team held brainstorming sessions to design engaging and educational modules on synthetic biology for children. The activity successfully attracted continuous young participants, many of whom were fascinated by the fluorescent bacteria, asked insightful questions, and shared their own knowledge. The event promoted interactive learning, documented methods for future reference, and inspired the next generation to engage with synthetic biology.

# High School Homecoming Seminar: Synthetic Biology for Teens

As part of our ongoing effort to promote synthetic biology education among younger generations, our iGEM team organized a special homecoming science seminar for high school students. Leveraging our project experience and educational resources, we designed an engaging lecture that introduced teens to cutting-edge biotechnologies such as gene editing and plasmid construction, while also sharing our current iGEM project on botulinum toxin detection.

![Introducing Synthetic Biology to High School Students](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/teens-education/figure-1.webp)

^1. Overview^

	We returned to our alma mater to deliver a seminar titled "From Gene Editing to Synthetic Biology: A Teen-Friendly Introduction." The session combined theoretical knowledge with practical examples, using multimedia tools to simplify complex concepts and inspire interest in synthetic biology.

^2. Key Content^

	^a.Introduction to Gene Editing Technologies^

		We introduced three generations of gene editing technologies—ZFN, TALEN, and CRISPR-Cas9—with a detailed explanation of the CRISPR-Cas9 mechanism. Comparisons between these technologies helped students understand their evolution and applications. We also explained gene knockout and knock-in strategies, highlighting the use of the URA3 marker gene in yeast and its application in negative selection with 5-fluoroorotic acid (5-FOA).

	^b.PCR and Plasmid Construction^

		We demonstrated how to obtain DNA fragments and linearized plasmid vectors via PCR, sharing practical primer design tips and characteristics of different PCR premixes. We also introduced the principles and applications of agarose gel electrophoresis. Additionally, we explained Gibson Assembly and overlap extension PCR as key techniques in plasmid construction.

	^c.Bacterial and Yeast Transformation^

		We outlined methods for preparing competent cells in E. coli and yeast, along with transformation protocols. We also detailed post-transformation validation techniques such as colony PCR, including primer design and specialized steps like alkaline lysis for yeast colony PCR.

	^d.DNA Sequencing Technologies^

		We compared three generations of DNA sequencing—Sanger sequencing, high-throughput sequencing, and single-molecule sequencing—explaining their principles, characteristics, and complementary applications in modern research.

	^e.iGEM Project: Yeast-Based Botulinum Toxin Detection^

		We presented our 2025 iGEM project, which uses a dual-yeast system to detect botulinum toxin without animal testing. This case study encouraged students to consider the real-world applications of synthetic biology in food safety and medical diagnostics.


^3. Impact and Reflection^

	Prior to the event, our team held multiple meetings to adapt complex scientific content into teen-friendly presentations. This careful preparation ensured the seminar was both educational and engaging, providing a valuable reference for future science outreach activities.

![Team Members Planning the High School Homecoming Seminar](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/teens-education/figure-4.webp)

	The students showed strong interest throughout the seminar. Many raised thoughtful questions about gene editing ethics, synthetic biology applications, and our iGEM project. Some expressed interest in pursuing biotechnology-related studies in the future.

	Through this homecoming seminar, we not only disseminated knowledge about synthetic biology but also inspired teens to think critically about science and innovation. We documented the entire process in detail, offering a replicable model for other iGEM teams and educational programs.

	We are confident that such initiatives will encourage more young people to learn about, engage with, and contribute to the field of synthetic biology, becoming the next generation of scientists and innovators.

^4. Student Feedback^

	a.Xue Junqiao: "I found the session on plasmid construction and biotechnology incredibly rewarding. The lecturer used personal research experiences to make the content vivid and practical. I not only filled knowledge gaps but also learned many new concepts. I hope to attend more such rich and engaging lectures in the future."

	b.Zhang Qifan: "The lecture was highly relevant to actual competition preparation. # Interdisciplinary Recruitment: Introducing Synthetic Biology to undergraduate

As part of our commitment to fostering interdisciplinary collaboration and promoting synthetic biology across academic fields, our iGEM team—through the Student Association of Coding Life—conducted a series of recruitment seminars for freshmen from various schools at the University of Science and Technology of China (USTC) in September 2025. These seminars deeply demystified synthetic biology, highlighted its engineering principles, and encouraged students from diverse backgrounds to join our association and participate in iGEM competitions.

^1.Overview^

	In September 2025, we delivered structured presentations in multiple schools across USTC, introducing synthetic biology as an interdisciplinary field that combines biology, engineering, and computer science. Through clear analogies and real-world examples, we explained how synthetic biology redesigns biological systems for applications in healthcare, environment, energy, and more.

![Club Overview and iGEM Competition Introduction](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/college-students-education/figure-1.webp)

^2.Key Content^

	^a.What is Synthetic Biology^

		We contrasted traditional biology—which studies natural systems—with synthetic biology, which builds new biological systems. We used examples like synthetic insulin, artificial genomes, and microbial production of artemisinin to show how synthetic biology moves from “reading” to “writing” life.

	^b.Engineering Principles: Decoupling, Standardization, and Modularization^

		We explained how synthetic biologists break down complex systems into functional modules, standardize biological parts (e.g., promoters, genes), and reassemble them like electronic circuits—enabling predictable, scalable biological design.

	^c.Introduction to iGEM^ 

		We introduced the iGEM (International Genetically Engineered Machine) events, emphasizing that these competitions are open to students from all majors. Our USTC iGEM team has been actively participating with significant achievements, providing members with opportunities for international exposure, academic development, and practical experience.

![USTC's Outstanding Achievements in iGEM Competition](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/college-students-education/figure-3.webp)


^3.Impact and Reflection^

	The seminars attracted students from schools including Life Sciences, Chemistry, Physics, Engineering, and Computer Science. Many participants expressed surprise at learning that synthetic biology is not limited to biology majors but involves skills from programming to design to social science. Faculty advisors also attended and expressed strong support for interdisciplinary collaboration.

	We documented the content and delivery of these seminars in detail, creating a reusable framework for future recruitment efforts. The emphasis on modularity, engineering, and real-world applications effectively broke down disciplinary barriers and encouraged students to see themselves as potential contributors to synthetic biology.

	We are confident that these outreach efforts have not only increased participation in iGEM but also helped build a more diverse, creative, and collaborative synthetic biology community at USTC.

# Campus Club Carnival: Synthetic Biology Outreach Booth for undergraduates

As part of the annual campus club carnival organized by the University of Science and Technology of China (USTC), our Student Association of Coding Life set up a dedicated synthetic biology outreach booth. With the support of posters, hands-on activities, and interactive demonstrations, we introduced synthetic biology to students and faculty across the university in an engaging and accessible way.

^1.Overview^

	We designed a compact yet impactful booth featuring three main activities: a DNA extraction experiment, a synthetic biology-inspired “collage poetry” workshop, and a QR-code-based quiz with prizes. Our volunteers facilitated each activity while an eye-catching roll-up banner provided background on synthetic biology and our association.

![Student Association Booth Setup at Campus Club Carnival](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/college-students-education/figure-5.webp)

^2. Key Activities^

	^a.DNA Extraction^

		Using bananas, dish soap, and ethanol, we demonstrated how to extract visible DNA strands. Participants could perform the experiment themselves under guidance, gaining a tangible understanding of genetic material.

![Live Demonstration of Banana DNA Extraction](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/college-students-education/figure-7.webp)

	^b.Synthetic Biology “Collage Poetry”^

		We compared genetic circuit design to poetry writing—both involving the creative assembly of modular elements. Using printed biological parts (promoters, genes, terminators) and Velcro boards, participants “designed” their own genetic systems. Successful designs earned prizes.

^3.Impact and Reflection^

	The booth attracted a continuous flow of students throughout the day, many of whom were from non-biology majors. Faculty members also stopped by to learn about our association, inquire about iGEM participation, and discuss potential collaborations. The event not only raised awareness about synthetic biology but also significantly increased the visibility of our association on campus.

	We documented the entire process—from activity design and material preparation to on-site execution—providing a reusable model for future student-led science outreach events. The low-cost, high-interaction format proved effective in bridging disciplinary gaps and sparking curiosity across the USTC community.

	We are confident that such well-documented, thoughtfully implemented activities will encourage more students to join synthetic biology initiatives, participate in iGEM, and contribute to a culture of open science and creativity at USTC and beyond.

# Dough Fermentation and an Introduction to Microorganisms:Synthetic Biology Hands-on Class for Adolescents with Autism

As part of our ongoing efforts to promote the popularization of synthetic biology education, we designed and conducted a special hands-on class for adolescents with autism, approximately 14 years old. Tailoring the content to their cognitive characteristics and interest preferences, we used dough fermentation as the central theme, guiding them to initially understand the basic concepts of microorganisms and genes through hands-on activities and visual aids.

^1. Key Content^

![](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/special-education/paint.webp)

    ^a.Dough Fermentation Practice^

    With one-on-one assistance from volunteers, the students personally made dough (using pre-packaged water, yeast powder, and flour) and let it rest briefly outdoors to observe the fermentation process. By touching the textures of noodles and bread, they were guided to feel the role of yeast in fermentation.

    ^b. Yeast-Balloon Model Experiment^

    By placing activated yeast with a glucose solution and some water into a small bottle and covering the mouth with a balloon, they observed the balloon inflating due to the carbon dioxide produced by the yeast, intuitively understanding the principle of yeast metabolism producing gas.

![](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/special-education/autism.webp)

    ^c.Drawing Pictures of Microorganisms^

    Students were encouraged to use their imagination to draw what they thought microorganisms looked like, enhancing the fun and engagement of the class.


    ^d.Introduction to Microorganism Knowledge^

    The lead instructor briefly introduced the basic concepts of microorganisms and, combined with the applications of yeast in daily life (such as fermented foods), extended the discussion to the practical significance of synthetic biology in everyday contexts.

^2. Learner Profiles and Teaching Adjustments^

    a. Most students possess basic communication skills; one individual repeated words but could understand instructions.

    b.Students have limited attention spans and are easily distracted, requiring aids like pictures and questions to assist understanding.

    c. Some students enjoy chatting with volunteers, watching animations, or drawing; these elements were incorporated into the teaching to enhance participation.

    d. Each student was accompanied throughout by a volunteer or a teacher from the institution to ensure operational safety and comprehension.

    e. The pace of asking questions needed to be slowed down, and key instructions required repeated reminders.

^3. Emergencies & Response^

    a. One student showed emotional instability during the PPT presentation, shouting loudly and waving their arms, but was later comforted by a volunteer and returned to the classroom.

    b. During the hands-on session, this student deliberately added an excessive amount of yeast; the volunteer provided one-on-one assistance and comfort, guiding them to complete the steps smoothly.

^4. Conclusion^

This specially tailored hands-on class, supported by continuous one-on-one companionship, introduced the fundamentals of synthetic biology into the cognitive world of adolescents with autism. We not only observed the children's genuine reactions and enthusiastic participation in scientific phenomena during the activity but also further confirmed the feasibility and significance of popular science education in synthetic biology for special needs groups. In the future, we will continue to optimize teaching content and methods, allowing the light of science to warm the hearts of more young people with diverse needs.

# An interview for  "Shao Nian Bo Lan" newspaper

To carry out biology popular science activities for primary and secondary school teenagers and enhance the social influence and popularity of synthetic biology and iGEM competitions, we were interviewed by Anhui Province's "*Shao Nian Bo Lan*" magazine. The "*Shao Nian Bo Lan*" magazine is mainly targeted at teenagers aged 8 to 16. 

![shao nian bo lan](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/teens-education/shao-nian-1.webp)

^1.Overview^

In the interview, we answered many basic biological questions and introduced a lot of knowledge about synthetic biology and the iGEM competition to the teenagers. Our answers were published in the knowledge-related section of the magazine.
	

^2.Key Content^

    ^a. Quick Question and Quick Answer:^
    
    We providing some answers to the biological knowledge popularization questions raised by the journalists, such as the number of human genes and the similarity of genes with other species, and popularizing some basic common sense of molecular biology for the teenagers.

    ^b. Discussion:^

![Discussion](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/hp/teens-education/shao-nian-2.webp)
    
    We discussed some topics that might interest teenagers, such as the current interesting applications of synthetic biology. At the same time, we also introduced some commonly used instruments in the laboratory to them and explained their uses in plain and understandable language. To enhance teenagers' interest in synthetic biology, we also introduced some synthetic biology experiments that they can conduct, such as fluorescent bacteria paintings, etc. We also shared some interesting stories that happened during experiments in the laboratory and the preparation of iGEM, as well as various activities we held, such as the gun tip insertion competition. To encourage them to engage in synthetic biology in the future, we have also roughly sketched out the path for them to participate in synthetic biology work in the future, and at the same time, inspired them with some famous people's deeds. We also introduced the reasons why we participated in iGEM, providing a reference for teenagers.

^3.Impact and Reflection^

    This interview with *Shao Nian Bo Lan* was a piece of targeted science outreach conducted at "a critical time for a key demographic." It successfully sowed the seeds of synthetic biology in the hearts and minds of young people, bridged the gap between cutting-edge technology and the public, and injected new vitality into the sustainable development of the iGEM competition. Its impact will grow increasingly apparent as these young readers mature, holding significant long-term value for advancing talent development and science popularization in the field of synthetic biology in China.
