# ^Background:^

+ ^1. What are BoNTs :^

    Botulinum neurotoxins (BoNTs) are the most lethal toxins known to mankind$^1$, which can cause botulism, a severe neuroparalytic disease that affects humans and vertebrate animals$^2$. Following their entry into the body, BoNTs are carried in the bloodstream to cholinergic synapses in the cranial nervous system. These toxins function by blocking acetylcholine (ACh) release to the peripheral nervous system and muscle nerve endings. This blockade causes paralysis that may lead to death from suffocation if it affects respiratory muscles. The lethal doses vary with the way the toxins enter into the body (as Figure 1) and about 50ng BoNTs can kill an adult.

    ![Figure 1 Estimated doses of BoNTs administered through various routes for an average adult human (70 kg) $^2$.](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/project/pdfig1.webp)  

    There are 7 serotypes of BoNTs: BoNT/A, BoNT/B, BoNT/C, BoNT/D, BoNT/E, BoNT/F, BoNT/G, produced by various strains of the anaerobic *Clostridium botulinum*, a kind of Gram-positive bacteria. Poisoning in humans is most often caused by strains A, B, E, and rarely F, with exposure mainly due to wound or ingestion. And among them, BoNT/A exhibits the highest toxicity and is the most common (both in daily exposure and frequency of use) type among BoNTs. It causes most of the botulism (as Figure 3) and showcases the highest market value, ^so our project focuses on BoNT/A.^

    ![Figure 2 Classification of *C. botulinum* and the other BoNT producing strains$^2$.](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/project/pdfig2.webp) 

    ![Figure 3 Reported botulism cases in the U.S. from 2001–2018 $^2$.](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/project/pdfig3.webp) 
    BoNT/A is a zinc-dependent metalloprotease (as other BoNTs) that enter motor neuron terminals, where it blocks ACh release by cleaving synaptosomal-associated protein 25 kDa (SNAP-25) that is essential for synaptic vesicle fusion. Temporary paralysis of adjacent muscles follows the disruption of the pathway, which causes the symptoms of botulism ranging from muscle weakness to respiratory failure. 

    Structurally, BoNT/A consists of two chains, heavy and light (100 and 50 kDa, respectively), linked by a disulfide bond. (as Figure 4) 

    ![Figure 4 The structure of BoNT including the two chains $^2$.](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/project/pdfig4.webp)

    The C- terminal end of the heavy chain (HCC) mediates entry of the toxin into neurons by binding to membrane receptors (synaptic vesicle protein 2, is short for SV2) while the N- terminal end of the heavy chain (HCN) is responsible for internalization into neuronal cell bodies. Upon entry of the toxin into the cell, the light chain (LC) functions as a metalloprotease that cleaves and inactivates the SNAP-25. (Figure 5) More specifically, the poisoning process of BoNT/A includes four stages:   
    1. Low affinity binding of the toxin, via its HCC region, to gangliosides, and high- affinity binding to protein receptors located on the membrane of the nerve cell;   
    2. Internalization of the full molecule into the cell via endocytosis;  
    3. Translocation of the LC domain through the endosome membrane;  
    4. Zinc- dependent endopeptidase cleavage of SNAP-25 by the LC fragment released into the neuronal cytosol.$^1$

    ![Figure 5 The toxicological mechanism of BoNT/A$^1$.](https://static.igem.wiki/teams/5924/project/pdfig5.webp)

+ ^2.The discovery and research history of BoNTs^

    The discovery of BoNTs and their producing organism *C. botulinum*, originated from pivotal outbreaks of foodborne botulism. In 1793, an early documented epidemic in Wildbad, Germany, resulted in 13 cases and 6 fatalities following consumption of contaminated sausages. Systematic characterization commenced in 1822, when Justinus Kerner published the first comprehensive clinical description based on this event and subsequent animal studies, notably proposing BoNTs' therapeutic potential. The etiological agent was isolated in 1895 by Émile van Ermengem from tainted ham during a Belgian outbreak; initially termed Bacillus botulinus, it was reclassified as *C. botulinum* in 1917. Early 20th-century research identified multiple BoNT serotypes.  
    Critical mechanistic insights emerged in 1964: Drachman demonstrated BoNT-induced denervation atrophy via blockade of acetylcholine release at neuromuscular junctions. Alan Scott later established clinical safety by confirming cardiac muscle resistance to intravenous BoNT in avian models, enabling its therapeutic application for strabismus, blepharospasm, and hemifacial spasms. Cosmetic utilization for wrinkle reduction subsequently expanded its scope.$^1$ It took about 2 centuries for human to gradually understand BoNTs, the most lethal toxins both causing panic over bioterrorism (because of its potential to be utilized as a biological weapon) and enhancing the health and well-being of humanity.

+ ^3.The medical and market value of BoNT/A^

    With the process of revealing the mechanism of BoNT/A, academic community has discovered the reversibility of muscle paralysis caused by botulinum toxin at safe doses, which indicated the potential medical value of this neurotoxin. And nowadays medical BoNT/A for injection is indeed widely used for medical use, especially cosmetic use. As mentioned above, BoNT/A can block the pathway of Ach releasing, resulting in muscle paresis or reduction of glandular secretions and thus has skin-enhancing benefits. In addition to the elimination of facial wrinkles, the cosmetic uses of BoNT/A include contouring of the lower face, neckline, and lower leg. It is also used to treat local hyperhidrosis of the axilla, forehead, palm, and scalp. Moreover, it is used for the management of gummy smile, facial asymmetry, and skin rejuvenation. Recently, novel attempts have been made to contour the lower face by using BoNTs to cause atrophy of the salivary glands.$^3$  
    The cosmetic effects of BoNT/A are being rapidly developed, and its market demand is also growing continuously after being launched into the market about 20 years ago. The global market size of BoNT/A in 2024 was approximately USD 8.65 billion to USD 12.24 billion. And the market size is expected to expand rapidly with a annual growth rate of 9.8%. (Figure 6)

+ ^4.The common causes of botulism^

    As mentioned above, the global market of medical BoNT/A for injection is expanding rapidly nowadays and the BoNT aesthetics services are getting more and more popular among the public. More common people have access to BoNT/A, which increases the risk of toxin exposure and even botulism due to illegal medical practice, non-conforming products, and improper dose control.

    Over a 10-year period, 161 cases of Cosmetic Iatrogenic Botulism (CIB) were identified, of which 80 (49.69%) were hospitalized and 81 (50.31%) were outpatient cases. Most patients received BoNT injections of unknown origin from unlicensed practitioners in nonmedical settings. Severe and moderate cases of CIB accounted for up to 60%, with the majority of outbreaks occurring in 2024. The median time from botulism onset was 0 to 17 days, with the highest incidence occurring within 3 days following the injection.$^5$ In the face of these cases, timely medical diagnosis and product testing are critical. In the fact, detection of medical BoNT/A for injection is gaining more attention nowadays and remains the statutory part throughout the entire chain. 

    Although CIB is a growing risk to people’s well-being, the infant botulism (usually caused by inhaling or ingesting substances containing *C. botulinum* spores) and foodborne botulism account for the most of the cases. (as Figure 8) This is caused by the extensive distribution of C. botulinum and its thermal- resistant spores (Figure 9), which are deemed only to be inactivated for 3 minutes at 121 ℃. *C. botulinum*spores (as Table 2), are widespread in nature and frequently isolated from soils, sediments and the intestinal tracts of animals. Subsequently, spores can enter the food chain through direct contamination of the animal or plant during production or through cross-contamination during harvest, handling, or processing. An average of 24% of inland soils tested in the United States was found to harbor *C. botulinum spores*. In analogous regions of Canada, Central and South America, Europe, and Asia the rate of isolation ranges from 1% to 30%, with the greatest prevalence near lakes, rivers, or coastal regions and areas prone to flooding. In sediment samples obtained from the Great Lakes area, cooler coastal regions in Alaska, Washington, Northern Europe (particularly near the Baltic region), China, and Taiwan, 30–95% were found to harbor *C. botulinum spores*, primarily strains of serotype E. Due to the pervasiveness of spores in coastal environments, marine and fresh water foods, such as fish, are often contaminated with type E *C. botulinum* spores. Type E spores were recovered from 56%, 55%, and 31% of fish intestine respectively from Green Bay, the South Baltic Sea, and the Columbia River. A survey conducted in France identified 7.8% of fish and shellfish used as ingredients for refrigerated foods as being positive for *C. botulinum* type A or B; when contaminated the average populations were 2–3 spores per kg. Not only in soil, rivers and fishery foods, the spores are also isolated from livestock products, vegetables like potatoes and turnip skins, mushrooms, Brussels sprouts, and cauliflower, and even processed foods (predominately preserved by sealed storage or anaerobic fermentation)$^6$.Moreover, spores can survive in the intestine and ceca of clinically healthy land animals, especially the underdeveloped intestines of children, which is a key factor of infant botulism.

    Each year about one thousand related cases of botulism were reported (and actually the number might greatly surpass that because of the limitation of epidemiological investigation). And the BoNT epidemic usually breaks out in clusters, especially the foodborne one. For example, since June 2024 a concentrated outbreak of foodborne botulism had occurred in multiple regions across Russia, mainly caused by the consumption of ready-to-eat salads with contaminated canned beans. As of July 11, a total 417 cases were confirmed, with 2 deaths.$^7$

    From the data and the cases, it is clear that botulism is not that far away from us in daily life and is even becoming more prevalent. For prevention of CIB, the medical products of BoNTs must be tested of content and quality; for timely medical diagnosis of poisoning, the fecal or plasma samples of sick infants or adults must be detected of BoNTs’ type and content. The detection of BoNTs, especially BoNT/A, is an important barrier to the cause of human health. 

+ ^5.The detection of BoNTs^

    As forementioned, the detection of this lethal toxin is necessary and strictly regulated by law from medical aesthetics to food safety industry. And BoNTs’ detection has a wide range of applications: clinical presentation of botulism, laboratory confirmation, food industry: survey of food safety and pharmaceutical industry uses (needs for precise quantification of active BoNT)$^8$

    Historically, BoNTs have been detected over many decades by the mouse bioassay (MBA), in which mice are injected with a dilution series of BoNTs using an appropriate stabilization buffer, and surviving or symptom-free mice are counted to determine the potency of this particular batch of BoNT$^9$.The MBA is still the primary method for the detection and quantification of BoNTs and is called “the golden standard” in the industry. That’ s because the MBA is the only BoNT detection assay that considers all aspects of BoNT intoxication, including steps involved in neuronal cell entry, and also pharmacodynamics of the toxins, thus reflecting the potency and serotypes of BoNTs, while other traditional methods mainly reflect its antigen integrity. For this the MBA is regarded as the most reliable detection method of BoNTs.

    But the MBA is not an optimal solution for many reasons. Firstly, the most important drawback of the MBA is the Animal Ethics Issues (Figure 10). Over the course of the test, mice will become unable to walk and eventually suffocate due to the failure of respiratory muscles. And during the test mice often die of starvation or dehydration because they are unable to move to their food bowls and water taps because of muscle paralysis. Both outcomes inflict great suffering on the mice. It has been estimated that over 400,000 mice have died annually across the EU for BoNT potency testing (and above 600,000 globally) during manufacturing $^10$, accounting for most of the mice consumed for regulation use. Secondly, it falls short to the quantitative capability and inter-laboratory standardization. The MBA exhibits a fairly low limit of detection(LOD), but since it can only rely on observing the toxic manifestations of mice and their time till death, it can only achieve semi-quantitation. Moreover, the lack of a unified toxicity measurement standard leads to variations in results obtained across different laboratories. Thirdly, the time and money costs also matter. Among different detection methods of BoNTs, the MBA is almost the most expensive and time-consuming (as Table 3).

    Many emerging physical-chemical and biological methods are more affordable and efficient, and even a few of them showcase better quantitative capability and limit of detection, but the reason they have not replaced the MBA yet is as what is mentioned above: most of them rely on antigen- antibody binding and thus are weaker in reflecting precise quantification of active BoNT, which exhibits its cleavage ability (potency), making the results less reliable. The methods that based on the physiological essence of BoNTs toxicity are usually more persuasive.

    In recent years academic community has paid more attention to biosensors, which is overall high-precision, rapid-response, high-specificity and environmentally friendly. Biosensors are gradually used in a series of fields including disease diagnosis, environmental protection, agricultural development and food safety. Some scientists are also attempting to achieve accurate detection of BoNT from the perspective of biosensors (Table 4). But unfortunately, most of these methods depend on precise but expensive optical or electronic instruments and the experimental operation is highly complicated, which makes it difficult to achieve technology dissemination in a relatively short period.

# Brief introduction:

To achieve detection of BoNT/A better in various aspects, our team proposed a project named Selective Proteolytic Botulinum Toxin Bioassay Based on Yeast (S.P.Y.). As this title, we designed a dual-yeast detection system (Figure 11), of which the limit of detection (LOD) can reach 2pg/mL based on preliminary modeling, with the MBA’s LOD about 10pg/ml. And different from most of the other methods, our detection system is based on the toxicological essence of BoNT/A, its proteolytic activity against SNAP-25, which is the same as the MBA and makes our method more reliable.

This system includes two edited strains of Saccharomyces cerevisiae BY4741: Substrate-acting Strain and Signal-transferring Strain.

Substrate-acting Strain is engineered to display SNAP-25-α-factor fusion protein on its cell wall. When BoNT/A exists, SNAP-25 is digested and the α-factor, a ligand acting on BY4741, is set free to trigger the generation of visual signal. 

Signal-transferring Strain is engineered to receive, amplify and transfer the signal of α-factor, which outputs vibrant light yellow Betalamic acid, the visual signal. And the signal will be further enhanced after adding some anime donors. 

In the occasion where there is BoNT/A, SNAP-25 is cut and α-factor is released from Substrate-acting Strain. Then the free α-factor acts on the Signal-transferring Strain and triggers it to produce Betalamic acid. Through the model built, the amount of pigment produced in a set time can reflect the concentration of BoNT/A of the sample.

Not only can this method produce quantitative results with promising precision, but it’s also easy to perform: simply mix the sample and this system. Our team also planned to turn this dual-yeast system into a commercialized detection kit. It’s our goal to improve the detection of BoNT/A for medical aesthetics, and what’s more, to bring our project S.P.Y. into the homes of ordinary people and contribute to the cause of people’s health.
